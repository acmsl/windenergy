Wind Energy Knowledge Map

A comprehensive structural view of wind energy technology

Mapping the entire discipline

ACMSL has created a comprehensive mind map covering all industrial aspects of wind energy. This knowledge map serves as both a learning guide and a reference tool, organizing the vast body of knowledge required by wind energy professionals into a clear, navigable structure.

The mind map is bilingual (Spanish/English) and contains hundreds of interconnected topics, from fundamental physics to project finance, from meteorology to safety regulations. It represents years of accumulated expertise distilled into a single, structured document.

The 11 branches of knowledge

1. Fundamentals & Context

The historical evolution of wind energy, current market status worldwide, the environmental context driving the energy transition, and the regulatory frameworks that shape the industry. Essential background for understanding why the sector operates the way it does.

2. Wind Resource

Wind resource assessment methodology, meteorological fundamentals, wind measurement techniques (anemometry, LiDAR, SODAR), wind data analysis, Weibull distributions, micrositing optimization, and wake effects. The foundation of every wind energy project.

3. Physical Principles

Aerodynamics of wind turbine blades (BEM theory, lift and drag, tip-speed ratio), electrical machine principles (induction and synchronous generators), and power electronics (converters, inverters, PWM modulation). The physics that makes wind energy conversion possible.

4. Power Generation & Distribution

Electrical grid characteristics, grid connection requirements, grid codes and compliance, energy storage technologies, power quality standards, and reactive power management. How wind energy integrates with the electrical system.

5. Instrumentation & Control

Industrial sensors and transducers, communication protocols (Modbus, OPC, IEC 61400-25), control algorithms (PID, state machines, MPPT), SCADA systems, and condition monitoring. The nervous system of modern wind turbines.

6. Wind Farms

Site evaluation and selection, project management and development, construction and commissioning, production estimation and optimization, operation and maintenance strategies, electrical substations, and balance of plant. The complete lifecycle of a wind farm project.

7. Wind Turbines

Design principles and IEC classification, the 5 generator families, major components (tower, blades, nacelle, hub, gearbox, generator, converter, foundation), advanced control strategies, and current manufacturer landscape. Deep technical knowledge of the machines themselves.

8. Weather Forecasting

Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models, mesoscale modeling, ensemble forecasting techniques, statistical downscaling, and forecast verification. Critical for grid integration and energy trading.

9. Economics & Finance

CAPEX and OPEX breakdown, Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) calculation, financial viability analysis, business models (PPA, merchant, feed-in tariffs), project financing structures, and investment risk assessment. The business case for wind energy.

10. Environmental & Social Aspects

Fauna impact assessment (birds, bats), noise emissions and propagation models, visual impact evaluation, Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) procedures, social acceptance, and community benefit schemes. Responsible development of wind energy.

11. Safety & Health

Safety norms and regulations, GWO (Global Wind Organisation) certification programs, Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) requirements, emergency procedures, risk assessment methodologies, and specific hazards of wind turbine work (heights, electrical, confined spaces, rescue). Keeping people safe in a demanding industrial environment.

About the knowledge map

The wind energy knowledge map was created using Freeplane, a free and open-source mind mapping application. The mind map format allows for hierarchical organization of topics with unlimited depth, making it ideal for representing the complex, interconnected nature of wind energy technology.

The map is continuously updated as the technology evolves, with new topics added to reflect the latest developments in offshore wind, grid-forming inverters, hydrogen production from wind, and other emerging areas.

A living document

The knowledge map is not a static reference — it is a living document that grows with the industry. Each branch contains dozens of sub-topics organized in a logical hierarchy, with cross-references between related areas. Whether you are a newcomer seeking an overview or an experienced professional looking for a specific technical detail, the knowledge map provides a structured path to the information you need.
